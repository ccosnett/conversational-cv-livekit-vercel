# Mastering the Wolfram Language

## Why this matters

This is not just "I learned a programming language." It sounds more like one of
the deepest formative technical experiences in the corpus.

It matters because it explains:

- why Conor is drawn to expressive tools
- why he values abstraction and compression
- why he dislikes unnecessary complexity in software
- how he learned to strip a problem down to its essential form

## Core claim

According to Conor's own account, mastering the Wolfram Language changed him
as a human being.

The reason is that the language gave him a way to represent programs in very
high-level, precise, and minimal mathematical notation.

That had a cognitive effect:

- it let him see programs concisely and succinctly
- it gave him a sense of crystalline purity and mathematical rigor
- it made him more sensitive to verbosity and unnecessary complexity in other
  languages

He contrasts this with languages that feel hairy or verbose, where the core
idea of the computation can get lost in the surrounding machinery.

## What it did to his mind

The most important distillation is:

- it helped him see computation more clearly
- it helped him recognize different forms of computation
- it trained him to get to the core of a matter
- it taught him to strip away incidental complexity
- it encouraged him to look for the simplest and most essential form of a
  problem

A particularly strong phrase from his answer is:

- "It allowed me to see the forest for the trees, the trees for the forest, and
  get to the core of a matter."

That is useful because it describes a general style of thought, not just a tool
preference.

## What this says about him as an engineer

- He is attracted to expressive technical mediums.
- He values terseness when it increases clarity.
- He is suspicious of accidental complexity.
- He likes tools that expose the underlying structure of a problem.
- He seems to care about elegance not as aesthetics alone, but as a way of
  thinking more truthfully about systems.

## Good reusable phrasing

- "Mastering the Wolfram Language changed how I think about computation."

- "It trained me to strip away complexity and look at a problem in its simplest
  essential form."
- "It made me care a lot about expressive power, precision, and representational
  clarity."
