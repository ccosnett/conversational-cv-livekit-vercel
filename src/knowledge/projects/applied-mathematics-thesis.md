# Applied Mathematics Thesis

## Why this matters

This appears to be one of the strongest "how Conor thinks" examples in the
corpus, not just a credential line.

It shows:

- mathematical generality
- comfort with formal systems
- ability to invent unusual computational representations
- deep self-directed learning

## Core thesis framing

The thesis was about SAT solvers, but the most personally meaningful part was
representing Conway's Game of Life in first-order logic and then converting
that representation into Boolean logic using the Wolfram Language.

What made that exciting was not only that it worked, but that the
representation was extremely general.

According to Conor's own account:

- it described all games of Conway's Life from one to infinity
- it captured all possible evolutions
- it captured all possible starting conditions

The thing he is proudest of is the mathematical generality of the
representation.

## Why he is proud of it

The key pride signal is not "I wrote a thesis" but:

- I found a very general formal representation of something interesting.
- I used symbolic and logical tools to describe an entire space of possible
  behaviors, not just one instance.
- I did this through an original computational process in the Wolfram Language.

This is important because it suggests Conor enjoys finding the right abstract
representation for a problem, then using that representation to reason at a
higher level.

## Pre-thesis preparation

An important part of the story is that the thesis did not come out of nowhere.

Beforehand, Conor spent roughly a year studying Wolfram Mathematica deeply and
trying to master it.

By his own account, he became strong enough in the language and ecosystem that:

- he developed unusually deep Wolfram Language expertise
- he answered many questions on Mathematica Stack Exchange
- that public expertise helped him later get hired by Wolfram Research

This matters because the thesis reflects a pattern:

- choose a powerful tool
- master it obsessively
- use it to think in a more general way
- turn that mastery into real-world leverage

## Why a software engineer should care

This thesis matters for software engineering not because it is an academic
credential, but because it demonstrates the kind of technical problem Conor is
comfortable with.

According to Conor's own framing:

- it was a hard scientific and technical problem
- it drew on mathematical logic, which is one of the foundations of computer
  science
- SAT solvers are not an isolated academic curiosity; their ideas show up in
  areas such as type checking and static analysis

The deeper point is that Conor sees mathematics, logic, and software
engineering as connected rather than separate domains.

This suggests a few useful interpretations:

- he values formal structure and correctness
- he is attracted to systems where the representation of the problem really
